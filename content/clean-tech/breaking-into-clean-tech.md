## Learning From Those Who've Done It

Breaking into a growing field sounds great in theory. But what does it actually look like? How long does it take? What obstacles come up that nobody warns you about? We spoke to professionals at different stages of their clean tech careers to find out what their paths really looked like — the wins, the detours, and the advice they wish they'd received earlier.

## From Mechanical Engineering to Solar Design

**Vikram, 26 — Solar Design Engineer at an EPC firm in Jaipur**

Vikram graduated with a B.Tech in Mechanical Engineering from a Tier-2 college in Rajasthan. He had no direct exposure to renewable energy during his degree.

"My college didn't offer a single elective in solar or wind energy. Everything I learned about renewables was self-taught — YouTube tutorials, free Coursera courses, and a lot of PVsyst practice."

His turning point was a 6-week online course in solar PV design, followed by the NABCEP Associate credential. "The NABCEP badge on my LinkedIn profile got me noticed. I had three interview calls within a month of posting about it."

His first role was as a junior design engineer at a small solar EPC company, earning ₹4.5 lakhs. Within two years, he moved to a larger firm at ₹8 lakhs, designing commercial rooftop systems across Rajasthan and Gujarat.

**His advice:** "Don't wait for your college to teach you this. The free resources are better than most curricula anyway. Learn PVsyst, get NABCEP Associate, and build a portfolio of design projects. That combination got me further than my degree alone."

## The Career Changer: Finance to ESG Consulting

**Priya, 30 — ESG Analyst at a Big Four consultancy in Mumbai**

Priya spent four years in corporate finance before pivoting to sustainability. The transition wasn't immediate — it took about 18 months of parallel learning while she kept her finance job.

"I started attending sustainability webinars and reading everything I could about ESG frameworks — GRI, SASB, TCFD. Then I enrolled in a 3-month course on carbon accounting through an online platform. The finance background turned out to be a huge asset because ESG reporting is fundamentally about measuring and disclosing — skills I already had."

She landed her first ESG role through a contact she made at a sustainability conference in Mumbai. Her starting salary was actually a 10% pay cut from her finance position, but within two years she'd surpassed her previous compensation.

"What I didn't expect was how much demand there is for people who understand both finance AND sustainability. The ability to calculate carbon footprints AND connect them to financial risk analysis makes you rare."

**Her advice:** "If you're switching from finance, consulting, or data analytics, don't think of it as starting over. You're adding a domain specialization to skills that are already valuable. The sustainability world needs more people who can talk numbers, not fewer."

## Fresh Graduate, No Connections

**Arun, 24 — Junior Wind Resource Analyst in Tamil Nadu**

Arun graduated with a B.Sc. in Physics from a small college in Coimbatore. He had no industry contacts and no family connections in the energy sector.

"I applied to 47 positions before getting my first response. The job market felt impossible at first because every posting seemed to want 2–3 years of experience. But I kept learning in the background — I taught myself WAsP using the student license and practiced wind data analysis with publicly available meteorological data from the India Meteorological Department."

He eventually got an internship at a wind energy consultancy through a referral from a professor who'd seen his weather data analysis project on GitHub. The unpaid 3-month internship converted to a full-time role at ₹5 lakhs.

"The GitHub portfolio was everything. When my interviewer asked about my wind analysis experience, I could pull up actual projects. It wasn't hypothetical — I'd analyzed real wind data, produced Weibull distributions, and generated energy yield estimates. That practical demonstration mattered more than my degree."

**His advice:** "If you don't have connections, build a portfolio that speaks for itself. Use public data — weather stations, government datasets, satellite data — and produce real analysis. When you show up to an interview with work you've already done, it changes the conversation completely."

## From IT to Green Hydrogen

**Sunita, 33 — Process Engineer at a green hydrogen pilot plant in Gujarat**

Sunita's path is unusual — she spent seven years as a software engineer at an IT services company in Pune before making a hard pivot to green hydrogen.

"I was comfortable in IT, but I felt disconnected from anything tangible. When I read about India's National Green Hydrogen Mission, something clicked. I went back to studying — part-time Master's in Chemical Engineering through IIT Bombay's distance program, combined with a Hydrogen Safety Engineering certification."

The transition took two years and involved a significant salary reduction initially. "I went from ₹18 lakhs in IT to ₹12 lakhs in my first hydrogen role. But within a year, I was at ₹20 lakhs, and the trajectory is steep because there are so few people with this combination of process engineering knowledge and real-world project experience."

**Her advice:** "Career changes into clean tech are absolutely possible, but be honest about the investment required. I spent two years learning and accepted a short-term pay cut. If you're not willing to do that, look for transition roles that bridge your existing skills — like clean tech software development or data analytics for energy companies. Those positions let you enter the sector without starting from scratch."

## The Rural Connection: Solar Technician to Entrepreneur

**Deepak, 28 — Solar installation business owner in Madhya Pradesh**

Deepak started as a solar panel installation technician after completing an ITI (Industrial Training Institute) course in electrician trade. He worked for a solar EPC company for three years, installing rooftop systems across small towns in MP.

"I realized that in Tier-3 towns, there was huge demand for solar but very few qualified installers. Customers were calling from 100 km away because nobody nearby could do the work properly."

He took a loan under the PM-KUSUM scheme support framework, got his electrician license upgraded, completed a solar-specific safety certification, and started his own installation business. He now employs four technicians and serves residential and small commercial customers across three districts.

"My monthly revenue now exceeds what I earned in a year as an employee. But the real change is seeing farming families reduce their electricity bills by 60–70%. That's the part they don't put in salary guides."

**His advice:** "Clean tech isn't only a career for engineers in metro cities. If you have electrical or mechanical skills and you're in a smaller city, the opportunity is enormous. The government schemes are real — PM-KUSUM has covered over 20 lakh farmers. Someone needs to install and maintain all those systems."

## Common Patterns Across These Stories

Several themes emerge from these diverse journeys.

**Self-learning matters more than credentials.** Every professional we spoke to invested heavily in self-directed learning — online courses, public datasets, open-source tools, YouTube tutorials. The industry values demonstrated capability over institutional pedigree.

**Portfolios beat resumes.** Whether it's a PVsyst design, a wind data analysis on GitHub, or a documented carbon footprint assessment, showing work you've actually done creates opportunities that a resume alone doesn't.

**The transition timeline is 6–24 months.** Nobody walked into clean tech overnight. Even Vikram, who came from a related engineering background, spent about 3 months building skills before landing his first role. Career changers like Priya and Sunita invested 18–24 months.

**Short-term pay cuts can lead to long-term gains.** Several professionals accepted initial salary reductions when entering the field, only to surpass their previous compensation within 1–2 years as their specialized skills became valuable.

**Location flexibility opens doors.** While metro cities offer higher absolute salaries, many clean tech opportunities — especially in installation, operations, and maintenance — are in states like Rajasthan, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu, and Madhya Pradesh, where cost of living is lower and talent competition is less intense.
